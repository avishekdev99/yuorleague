Mavericks Land Klay Thompson, But Defensive Issues Remain

The Dallas Mavericks, fresh off their first NBA Finals appearance since 2011, have been aggressively active in free agency, aiming to bolster their roster for another championship run. After falling short against the Boston Celtics, the Mavericks signed forward Naji Marshall, traded Tim Hardaway Jr. to the Pistons for young guard Quentin Grimes, and reportedly acquired four-time champion Klay Thompson in a multi-team sign-and-trade deal with the Golden State Warriors.

Assuming the Thompson deal goes through, the Mavericks' projected starting lineup for next season will be:

  • Luka Doncic
  • Kyrie Irving
  • Klay Thompson
  • PJ Washington
  • Dereck Lively II

On paper, this lineup boasts significant talent. Doncic and Irving have already proven they can coexist and lead a team to the Finals. Lively’s impressive rookie season earns him a starting spot, and Washington showed his prowess on both ends during the postseason. Adding Thompson’s caliber to the mix makes this lineup formidable most nights.

However, free agency has its downsides. The Mavericks lost Derrick Jones Jr., their best defender last season, to the Clippers, and traded Josh Green in the Thompson deal. While Green's departure isn't a significant loss, swapping Jones and Green for Marshall and Thompson doesn’t seem like the major upgrade it should be.

This critique isn't about Thompson's abilities but rather the issues he doesn’t solve for the Mavericks. Against the Celtics, the Mavericks struggled defensively against multiple perimeter threats. Even with Washington and Jones, they lacked a strong point-of-attack defender to disrupt Boston's perimeter actions. Thompson, post-injuries, isn’t the elite defender he once was. While still good, he isn’t great, and with weaker defenders like Doncic and Irving, the Mavericks need Thompson to perform closer to his prime defensive years—an unrealistic expectation.

Offensively, Thompson is a clear upgrade, especially as a shooter. Despite a down year, he still shot 38.7% from deep on nine attempts per game, which would have ranked second on the Mavericks. However, adjusting to Dallas’s heliocentric offense, which relies on Doncic and Irving, may be challenging for Thompson, who is used to Golden State’s ball-movement system.

Thompson’s role in Dallas will likely see him as a catch-and-shoot player, waiting in the corners for passes from Doncic and Irving. While effective, this is a departure from his previous role. He was convinced by the Mavericks' pitch, so there may be adjustments or compromises in their offensive system. The Mavericks have used off-ball screens effectively before, and Thompson could thrive in that role.

Beyond being a 3-point shooter, Thompson can score off the dribble, addressing a Mavericks' need from their Finals clash with the Celtics, where Irving and Doncic carried too much of the offensive load. However, expanding this part of his game will be an adjustment.

The primary concern remains the Mavericks’ defense. Thompson, despite his offensive prowess, doesn’t solve their defensive weaknesses. Unless another trade is in the works, Dallas will heavily rely on Thompson’s defense—a significant gamble. Retaining Jones Jr. and acquiring Marshall might have been a safer bet. However, the Mavericks have taken risks before, such as trading for Irving, that paid off. Given they gave up little to get Thompson and received some second-round picks, this might be a risk worth taking.
